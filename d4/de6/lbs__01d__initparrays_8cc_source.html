13 /**Initializes p_arrays.\n
14 The question arises of what datatype can store the total amount of
15 unknowns. For now we will say we want to be
16 designing for 100 billion cells with
17 an assumed shape of a truncated octahedron which has 24 vertices.
18 We will also assume that we will be able to do 2000 energy groups
19 and finally we will assume we will do scattering orders up to 16
20 which requires 289 moments.
21  DOFS per truncated octahedron = 24\n
22  Energy groups = 2000\n
23  Moments = 289\n
24  # of cells = 100,000,000,000\n
25  Total DOFS = 2,400,000,000,000\n
26  Unknowns per cell = 13,872,000\n
27  Total Unknowns = A crap ton\n
28 \n
29 It is easy to see here that this is a hell of a lot so how about we think about
30 something more modest. Like 200 energy groups scattering order 5 (36 moments)
31 and 2 billion cells.\n
32  Energy groups = 200\n
33  Moments = 36\n
34  # of cells = 2,000,000,000\n
35  Total DOFS = 48,000,000,000\n
36  Unknowns per cell = 7,200\n
37  Total Unknowns = 1.44xe13\n
38 \n
39 A long int only supports up to 4.29e9. This obviously requires
40 unsigned long long int which can hold up to 2x2e63.\n
41 \n
42 Another interesting aspect is what it will take to get to exascale. For a
43 discrete ordinates code this will undoubtly be evident in the amount of angular flux
44 unknowns. 1 billion cells, 24 vertices, 200 groups, 48 azimuthal angles per
45 octant, 8 polar angles per octant (3072) angles. 1.47456e16. Just a factor 68
46 away from exascale.
47  */
